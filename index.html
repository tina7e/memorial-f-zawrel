Introduction

This page is dedicated to the memory of Friedrich Zawrel, whose life story serves witness to the cruelty of medicine and psychia­try under the ideology of "racial hygiene". It further illustrates that this ideology permeates the medical field far beyond 1945 al­low­ing careers begun under its premise to prosper into our cur­rent millennium. Above all, it shows the necessity and impor­tance of continued resistance.


Childhood

Friedrich Zawrel's childhood in Vienna is overshadowed by bitter poverty and the alcohol addiction of his father. Early on he is ta­ken into foster care and spends periods in educational institu­tions. During a short-lived re­turn to the family setting in 1940, Friedrich receives a letter that excludes him from joining any NSDAP organizations because of his father's exemption to serve.


When he doesn't join the Hitler Youth, Friedrich is ostracized and bullied by his classmates. He recounts that the other boys wagered that the reason for his exclusion couldn't be that he was Jewish because in that case "he'd be in a concentration camp". In his later years, Zawrel would point out how improbable it was that the general population wasn't aware of the Holocaust, when even school children knew about it.


Am Spiegelgrund

In 1941, Friedrich Zawrel is taken into custody again and transferred to the municipal educational facility "Am Spiegelgrund" where he spends most of the next three years in an isolation cell. He witnesses other patients including children disappearing over night while the resident doctors use him to demonstrate to prospective nurses which of his bodily features make him "lebensunwert" ("unworthy of life"). Am-Spiegelgrund doctor Heinrich Gross tortures Zawrel with "medical" experiments and declares him "hereditarily and socially inferior". In 1944, a nurse helps Friedrich Zawrel flee the institution.


"Am Spiegelgrund" was a youth welfare institution on the premises of the medical and care facility "Am Steinhof" in Wien between 1940 and 1944. The facility comprised an educational home and a psychiatric clinic for children, including a "child specialist department", where sick, disabled and "non-educable" children were exposed to medical experiments and torture. At least 789 children were murdered.


Meeting Doctor Gross again

In the following decades, Friedrich Zawrel struggles to adjust to society and gets caught up in petty crime. 30 years after the war, he is being tried for property offenses and finds himself facing Am-Spiegelgrund doctor Heinrich Gross at court. Gross had built an impressive career on the base of his "research" at Am Spiegelgrund and is at this time the most requested court psychiatrist in Austria.


Facing Gross at court, Zawrel says: "Believe me, I know people, who have committed crimes hundreds of thousands times worse than I, but they are respected persons today, that is in high positions and such."


Gross doesn't understand nor recognize Zawrel, and asks if he has ever been committed to psychiatric care before. Zawrel replies: "Herr Doktor, for an academic, you do have a very bad memory. [...] Herr Doktor, can you even sleep well anymore? Have you already forgotten, the many dead children of Pavillon 15, have you already forgotten the tortured and abused children of Pavillon 17?"


Court psychiatrist Gross claims not to be biased. In the psychiatric appraisal of Zawrel that he composes for the court, he then recommends that Friedrich Zawrel be imprisoned permanently in an instutition for dangerous repeat offenders. He bases this appraisal on a psychiatric evaluation that his Am Spiegelgrund colleague Ernst Illing had written in 1943. It attested that Friedrich Zawrel came from "a genetically and sociologically inferior family", "a criminal, asocial tribe". Illing had been hanged in 1946 for about 200 murders committed at Am Spiegelgrund.
